  • Worked with the Game Designer, Lead Artist, Lead Programmer and team producers to lead the creation of the game
  • Communicated game design between leadership group and level designers
  • Created level design templates for the track design team and maintained level design documentation on the game's wiki

  • Implemented AI driving paths, checkpoints, kart spawn points, and respawn volumes for 3 tracks

Leadership Work

Level Design Documentation

One of my biggest responsibilities as the Lead Level Designer was to help the designers create useful documentation for their levels and features and to help maintain that documentation.

I created an initial LDD template for the track designers to document pertinant information about their track designs, including assets and functionality they would need from other discplines.

As the project progressed, we as a leads team decided to migrate all of our documentation to a wiki, very similar to confluence, so we had documentation everyone on the team had easy access to.

Technical Design Work

Swinging Pirate Ship

In Go Go-Karts, each of the three tracks had a set of track specific environmental hazards.

For the Pirate track, I created the initial functionality for the swinging pirate ship that could knock players out of the air.

I made the ship swing by lerping the mesh's rotation between two values over a duration based on a timeline curve.

I made two variables public: "Swing Height" and "Swing Speed Multiplier". This allowed the track designers to control how high and how fast the ship would swing, so they could tune the ship based on playtests of the track.

Track AI Splines

As a technical designer on the track and environment team, one of my main responsibilities was to set up the AI Splines for each track. The AI Splines were what allowed the AI racers to drive on the track and compete in the races.

I utilized a tool made by our AI team that let me make different spline sections for each track. The basic process of this tool was to create a spline section, define the width of its lanes, and add new points to the section to mirror the shape of the track. The tool was very robust in that I had complete control over the spline shape just by editing the transform data of any point.

The tool also allowed me to create split paths from one path as well as merge split paths back into the main path. This way, the AI racers would take shortcuts and different paths based on factors definied by the AI team.

I could select an existing segment I wanted to split from or merge to, and the tool would automatically fill in the information for "Known Splits". However, I did have the control to go back and manually edit this data if I wanted to change where on a spline the split or merge occured.

Post Mortem

What Went Well
  • As the lead level designer, I felt that I communicated my designers’ concerns to the leads group and stakeholders well
  • After I stepped down from the lead level designer position, as a technical designer, I was able to communicate well with the programmers and understand the systems they had designed
    • I was able to implement required technical systems (AI Splines, Checkpoints, Respawn Volumes) in each track without much trouble
  • With my past professional experience in QA I was able to help the QA producer when she was creating a QA test plan and assits other teammates be efficient and detailed testers
What Went Wrong
  • As a lead, I didn’t see issues in the track and environment pipeline process early enough, and subsequently we as a leads group were not able to address those issues; as a result, we cut one of the tracks from the game due to time constraints
  • Since we did not have point people on sub-teams, I had a difficult time knowing exactly what progress each team was making and what issues they were running into because I had to check in with some many individuals every single day
  • I took personal responsibility for every person’s grievances that were voiced to me and tried to satisfy everyone. As a result, I ended up stressing myself out more than I should have
What I Learned
  • Having a point person on a sub team who is close to the ground and knows that team’s daily issues can help me be more aware of each team’s progress
  • Stepping down from a position does not equal failure; Its realistically acknowledging your limits and recognizing what would be best for the team and project
  • You have to find a balance between contributing your best efforts to a project while also taking care of yourself
